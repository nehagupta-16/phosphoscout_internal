Overview

What the variant is and why it's being analyzed

FieldValue
GeneMYC
Mutation (CDS)
Mutation (AA)p.P74S

MYC p.P74S (PRO74SER) resides in the N‑terminal transactivation domain of c‑Myc, a region critical for transcriptional regulation and oncogenic function [4]. The variant is cataloged as a somatic mutation in oncology knowledgebases and has been observed in a relapsed/refractory aggressive B‑cell lymphoma case report [1, 2]. The surrounding N‑terminal region harbors multiple experimentally annotated phosphosites, and within the analyzed 29‑mer, EPSD documents S73, S77, and S79 as phosphosites proximal to the P74 position [7]. Reviews of MYC biology emphasize that phosphorylation within the N‑terminus contributes to MYC’s regulation of proliferation and apoptosis, underscoring the functional relevance of phosphosite changes in this region [5, 6].

Hypotheses

Mechanistic explanations generated from the overview and literature.

MYC p.P74S creates a new CDK/MAPK/GSK3-recognized phospho-degron at S74 that functionally competes with canonical MYC MB1 degron control, increasing MYC oncogenic output in aggressive B-cell lymphoma

The MYC p.P74S substitution creates a new serine phospho-acceptor at center_pos S74 within the N-terminal transactivation/Myc Box region of MYC [4, 7]. Kinase–substrate specificity is predicted to be strongly rewired toward gain of recognition at S74 by multiple proline-directed kinases including CDK2/CDK7/CDK9 and MAPK1/ERK2 (all gains), and also by GSK3A/GSK3B (gains), relative to wildtype where S74 does not exist [7]. Because MYC turnover and transactivation are regulated by phosphorylation-dependent degron logic in the nearby N-terminus that interfaces with SCF ubiquitin ligases (including FBW7-mediated degradation driven by phospho-degron recognition) [8], the new predicted S74 phospho-degron could (testably) reroute which kinases “license” MYC for ubiquitin-mediated proteolysis versus sustained transcriptional activity, thereby promoting MYC-driven proliferation programs in aggressive B-cell lymphoma where this variant has been observed [1, 2].

Disease context

  • Relapsed/refractory aggressive B-cell lymphoma (observed case context) [2]
  • Cancer contexts with aberrant MYC activity (broader relevance) [3]

Predicted observations

  • Phospho-readout: targeted PRM/SRM mass spectrometry detects phosphorylation on the new site pS74 in MYC p.P74S but not in MYC WT, with site localization confidence sufficient to distinguish pS74 from nearby pS73/pS77/pS79 [7].
  • Kinase dependency shift: acute inhibition/knockdown of CDK7 or CDK9 reduces the pS74 signal more strongly in p.P74S cells than in WT cells (directionality to be empirically determined) [7].
  • Mechanism-discriminating readout: compared with WT MYC, p.P74S shows altered MYC protein half-life and/or altered FBW7 co-immunoprecipitation in a manner that is specifically lost when S74 is reverted/ablated (P74S/S74A) (direction to be tested) [8].
  • Downstream phenotype: in lymphoma-relevant cells, p.P74S increases MYC-dependent transcriptional output and proliferation that is selectively blunted by S74A editing or by the kinase perturbation that most strongly controls pS74 (CDK7/CDK9 vs MAPK1 vs GSK3), enabling pathway attribution [2, 5, 8].
Laboratory validation

Suggested models

  • Aggressive B-cell lymphoma cell lines with high MYC activity (engineered knock-in of MYC p.P74S at the endogenous locus) [2, 5].
  • Isogenic HEK293T or similar tractable lines expressing MYC WT vs p.P74S from the same promoter for mechanistic biochemistry (FBW7 binding, half-life) [8].

Assays

  • Generate isogenic variants: MYC WT, MYC p.P74S, and MYC p.P74S/S74A (site-dead) (plus optional S74D/E phosphomimetic) and confirm comparable MYC expression [4].
  • Phosphosite measurement: targeted phosphoproteomics (PRM) for pS74 and nearby sites (pS73/pS77/pS79) to establish whether S74 is phosphorylated and whether local phosphorylation patterns shift (directionality measured, not assumed) [7].
  • Kinase perturbation: treat with selective CDK7 and CDK9 inhibitors and/or CRISPRi/siRNA of CDK7/CDK9; measure effects on pS74 and on MYC half-life and MYC target transcription [5, 8].
  • Ubiquitin–proteasome axis test: assess MYC ubiquitination and MYC–FBW7 interaction by co-IP ± proteasome inhibitor, and test epistasis by FBW7 knockdown; determine whether any p.P74S phenotype is FBW7-dependent and S74-dependent (rescue/epistasis) [8].

Controls and confounders

  • Confirm that any inhibitor effects are not due to global transcription shutdown by measuring short-lived control transcripts/proteins and normalizing to MYC mRNA where appropriate [5].
  • Include MYC P74S with S74A as the key causal control to attribute phenotypes to the new phosphosite rather than other local structural effects [4].
  • Measure phosphorylation at canonical MYC degron sites (e.g., Thr58/Ser62) in parallel by phospho-specific immunoblot or MS to distinguish ‘new S74 phospho-degron’ effects from canonical MB1 regulation [8].

Feasibility notes

A pS74-specific antibody may not exist; MS-based targeted quantification is the primary phospho readout, with antibody development as a follow-on if the site is validated [7].

Limitations
  • Kinase-event outputs represent predicted kinase recognition rewiring, not measured phosphorylation occupancy at S74; phosphorylation directionality and magnitude require phospho-proteomic validation [7].
  • FBW7 recognition is established for MYC via a defined phospho-degron logic, but whether the new S74 site forms a functional degron or interferes with existing degron processing is unproven and could be context-dependent [8].
  • Disease linkage is currently supported by a case observation and knowledgebase cataloging rather than a cohort-level statistical association for P74S specifically [1, 2].
