Overview

What the variant is and why it's being analyzed

FieldValue
GeneKDM6A
Mutation (CDS)
Mutation (AA)p.I724T

KDM6A p.I724T (Ile724Thr) lies within the N-terminal TPR-containing region of UTX, a histone H3K27 demethylase in COMPASS-like complexes that is recurrently altered in cancer and associated with Kabuki syndrome 2 when mutated in the germline [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. In our phosphosite-window analysis centered on residue 724, the mutation introduces a new central threonine phospho-acceptor in an evidence-supported window, and the site is solvent-accessible (RSA ≥ 0.1, ±3 positions). Kinase-specificity scoring on this window indicates substantial increases for NEK1 and MAP3K20 on the mutant versus wild type segment.

Hypotheses

Mechanistic explanations generated from the overview and literature.

KDM6A p.I724T creates a new phospho-acceptor (T724) with predicted NEK1/MAP3K20(ZAK) recognition, rewiring UTX enhancer control toward stress/DNA-damage programs in cancer

KDM6A/UTX is a COMPASS-like complex component that regulates enhancer chromatin via H3K27 demethylation and is recurrently inactivated in cancers (notably urothelial carcinoma) [1]. The p.I724T substitution introduces a new solvent-accessible phospho-acceptor at center_pos T724 (created by mutation) and is predicted to cause kinase-substrate specificity gains for MAP3K20/ZAK (gain) and NEK1 (gain) on the local window, relative to wild type [1, 4]. We hypothesize that this newly created, regulatable T724 site enables stress/DNA-damage signaling kinases (ZAK→p38/JNK; NEK1 in homologous recombination/replication fork stability) to modulate KDM6A’s protein–protein interactions in its TPR region, shifting KDM6A chromatin engagement and enhancer output toward stress-adaptation programs that support tumor cell fitness [1, 8, 9]. This is predicted to manifest as kinase-dependent changes in KDM6A occupancy and H3K27me3/H3K27ac balance at a subset of regulatory elements rather than a constitutive loss of KDM6A expression or catalytic activity [1, 4].

Disease context

  • Urothelial carcinoma / bladder cancer (KDM6A frequently mutated; tumor suppressor context) [1]
  • Gastrointestinal cancers with KDM6A gatekeeper roles (epigenetic control of growth pathways) [2]

Predicted observations

  • Phospho-readout (directionality test): targeted phosphoproteomics (PRM/SRM) or a custom phospho-specific antibody will detect phosphorylation at KDM6A T724 in p.I724T cells but not WT, and its abundance will be acutely sensitive to ZAK (MAP3K20) or NEK1 perturbation (inhibitor or genetic knockdown), establishing kinase dependence rather than assuming phosphorylation direction a priori [8, 9].
  • Mechanism-discriminating dependency shift: compared with WT KDM6A, p.I724T cells will show a stronger functional coupling to stress-kinase signaling—e.g., greater sensitivity (or altered adaptive response) to ZAK-pathway blockade during ribotoxic stress (anisomycin) measured by p38/JNK activation markers and clonogenic survival, consistent with a new ZAK-recognized site on KDM6A [8].
  • Chromatin consequence: p.I724T will produce a kinase-dependent redistribution of KDM6A chromatin occupancy (CUT&RUN/CUT&Tag for KDM6A) accompanied by matched changes in H3K27me3 and/or H3K27ac at affected enhancers (CUT&Tag/ChIP), without necessarily changing global KDM6A protein levels, consistent with a regulatory (not truncating) N-terminal event [1, 4].
  • Protein-interaction readout: co-immunoprecipitation or proximity labeling (BioID/TurboID) will show altered association of p.I724T KDM6A with COMPASS-like partners (e.g., MLL3/4 complex components) in a manner that is reduced by blocking T724 phosphorylation (T724A in the p.I724T background) [1].
Laboratory validation

Suggested models

  • Urothelial carcinoma cell lines with endogenous KDM6A alteration complemented with WT vs p.I724T KDM6A knock-in/knockout-rescue [1]
  • GI cancer models where KDM6A acts as an epigenetic gatekeeper for growth control [2]

Assays

  • Generate isogenic lines: (i) WT KDM6A, (ii) p.I724T, (iii) phospho-dead T724A (in the p.I724T-created site context), and (iv) phosphomimetic T724E (in the p.I724T context), then compare phenotypes and chromatin outputs [1].
  • Site-level phospho validation: PRM/SRM phosphoproteomics for the T724-containing peptide ± acute ribotoxic stress; validate kinase dependence with MAP3K20/ZAK and NEK1 perturbation (CRISPRi/siRNA and/or selective pathway inhibitors where available) [8, 9].
  • Epistasis/rescue: test whether (a) ZAK/NEK1 perturbation effects on KDM6A chromatin occupancy and H3K27me3/H3K27ac are present in p.I724T but lost in T724A (epistasis: site required), and (b) T724E partially bypasses kinase perturbation on chromatin/phenotype (rescue: phosphorylation state sufficient) [1, 8].
  • Chromatin readouts: CUT&Tag/CUT&RUN for KDM6A, H3K27me3, H3K27ac; ATAC-seq for enhancer accessibility changes linked to KDM6A reprogramming [1].
  • Phenotype: clonogenic growth under stress (anisomycin/ribotoxic agents; replication stress), cell-cycle checkpoints, and DNA damage markers to connect stress kinase signaling to fitness outcomes in a KDM6A-dependent manner [8, 9].

Controls and confounders

  • Control for KDM6A catalytic contribution by including a JmjC-inactive KDM6A rescue (demethylase-dead) to distinguish interaction/regulatory effects from catalytic loss-of-function [1].
  • Normalize for X-linked dosage/sex effects and confirm comparable KDM6A protein expression across isogenic lines to attribute effects to T724 regulation rather than abundance changes [4, 6].
  • Use kinase-activity markers (p38/JNK phosphorylation for ZAK axis; HR/fork stability proxies for NEK1 axis) to verify on-target pathway modulation during perturbation experiments [8, 9].

Feasibility notes

T724 is a mutation-created site, enabling clean causal tests via T724A/T724E in the p.I724T background and kinase perturbation epistasis to separate 'binding preference rewiring' from downstream chromatin consequences [1, 4].

Limitations
  • Kinase events represent predicted specificity gains (MAP3K20/NEK1) and do not by themselves establish in-cell phosphorylation at T724; site phosphorylation must be empirically verified and its directionality quantified [4].
  • Direct biochemical evidence that ZAK or NEK1 phosphorylates KDM6A is not established here; kinase–substrate linkage requires in vitro kinase assays or cellular kinase-dependence mapping [8, 9].
  • Residue 724 lies in the TPR region where effects may be mediated via altered protein–protein interactions; identifying the specific affected KDM6A partners may require unbiased interactomics [1, 5].
