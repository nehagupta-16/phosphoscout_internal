Overview

What the variant is and why it's being analyzed

FieldValue
GeneBCL6
Mutation (CDS)
Mutation (AA)p.N389S

BCL6 p.N389S (ASN389SER) maps within a serine-rich region and introduces a serine at position 389, creating a potential S/T phospho-acceptor at this site [8]. Within the local 29-mer, we detected an evidence-supported phosphosite at S385 (known in PhosphoSitePlus) and a mutation-created S389 site; both are solvent-accessible by AlphaFold/DSSP-based RSA (≥0.1 within ±3) [5, 6, 7, 8]. BCL6’s role as a GC master regulator implicated in lymphoma underscores the potential regulatory impact of altered phosphorylation in this region [1, 2]. Predicted kinase specificity indicates substantial gains for NEK-family serine/threonine kinases at the S389-gain window (mutant > wildtype) [8].

Hypotheses

Mechanistic explanations generated from the overview and literature.

BCL6 p.N389S creates a NEK-family-rewired phospho-switch at S389 that stabilizes BCL6 and sustains GC-like transcriptional repression in DLBCL

The BCL6 p.N389S substitution introduces a new serine phospho-acceptor at center_pos 389 within the serine/proline-rich regulatory region of BCL6 [8]. Kinase–substrate specificity modeling predicts a gain of NEK-family recognition at the new S389-centered motif (NEK2/NEK5/NEK6/NEK7/NEK9; direction=gain), implying that the mutant sequence may be more efficiently targeted by these kinases than wild-type in cells where they are active [8]. Because phosphorylation within BCL6’s serine/proline-clustered region is known to couple antigen receptor/MAPK signaling to BCL6 turnover (phosphorylation→degradation axis), a NEK-directed phosphorylation program at S389 is hypothesized to compete with or re-time the established MAPK-linked degradation control, shifting BCL6 toward increased stability and prolonged transcriptional repression in GC-derived lymphomas (prediction requires direct pS389 and BCL6 half-life readouts) [1, 3, 4]. This would be disease-relevant in germinal-center–origin lymphomas where sustained BCL6 activity supports the GC-like state and lymphomagenesis [1, 2].

Disease context

  • Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (germinal-center–derived / GC-like programs) [1, 2]

Predicted observations

  • Targeted phosphoproteomics or phospho-specific antibody detects BCL6 pS389 in p.N389S cells but not WT [8].
  • In vitro kinase assays show higher NEK7/NEK6/NEK2 activity toward N389S peptide than WT [8].
  • BCL6 p.N389S alters BCL6 half-life/ubiquitination vs WT in a manner correlated with pS389 abundance [3, 4].
  • NEK inhibition/knockdown reduces pS389 and shifts BCL6 abundance/phenotypes preferentially in N389S vs WT [8].
Laboratory validation

Suggested models

  • GC-like DLBCL cell lines with BCL6-WT vs BCL6-N389S (CRISPR knock-in or matched inducible).
  • HEK293T or BJAB/Ramos for rapid kinase/turnover assays; confirm in lymphoma models.

Assays

  • Targeted MS (PRM) for S389 phosphopeptide; include nearby sites as controls.
  • In vitro kinase assays (NEK2/6/7/9) with WT vs N389S peptides (382–396).
  • Cycloheximide chase and ubiquitination IP for BCL6 stability.
  • Epistasis with N389A/N389D(E) mutants; NEK7 CRISPRi/siRNA ± rescue; MAPK-pathway perturbation comparator.

Controls and confounders

  • Match BCL6 expression/localization across conditions.
  • Include kinase-dead NEK7 rescue where relevant to parse scaffolding vs catalytic roles.
  • Control for cell-cycle effects on NEK-family activity.

Feasibility notes

Targeted MS provides the cleanest initial detection for a mutation-created site (N389S).

Limitations
  • NEK-family signals are predicted specificity gains; pS389 must be empirically verified [8].
  • No direct literature linking NEKs to BCL6 yet; inference leverages BCL6 phospho-turnover biology [3, 4].
  • Functional direction (stabilizing vs destabilizing) requires half-life/ubiquitination assays.

Interpretation
Context

Homo sapiens lymph node; comparison = diffuse large B-cell lymphoma vs normal; tissue+disease-only fallback (no cell-type filter).

Mechanistic expectations
  • If BCL6 p.N389S stabilizes BCL6 and rewires to NEK2/5/6/7/9, lymphoma samples should show higher BCL6 and increased NEK-family kinase expression, consistent with sustained GC-like repression.
Observed results
  • BCL6 up (logFC 0.64, ES 1.27, q 0.021)
  • NEK6 strongly up (logFC 1.07, ES 2.83, q 0.021)
  • NEK9 up (logFC 0.14, ES 0.31, q 0.025)
  • NEK2 small logFC but large ES (0.92), q 0.025
  • NEK5 ~no meaningful change (tiny negative ES −0.08 despite q 0.023)
  • NEK7 no change (q 0.50).
Alignment assessment
  • Multiple hypothesis genes show coordinated, non-trivial increases (BCL6, NEK6, NEK9; NEK2 by ES), matching gain-of-function direction; two family members (NEK5/NEK7) do not increase, but lack of change does not contradict a PTM-centric mechanism
  • Effects observed despite broad cell-type mixture, suggesting robust signal in lymphoma context.
Conclusion

SUPPORTS — scRNA patterns in DLBCL lymph node show BCL6 and key NEKs (notably NEK6/NEK9) upregulated vs normal, consistent with a NEK-rewired, stabilized BCL6 program; confidence = medium (fallback context; PTM mechanism may not fully manifest at mRNA).
